1 Taylor Series

1.1 What are derivatives?

The derivative \(f'(x)\) of \(f(x)\) is the slope at the point x.
For example we can determine if \(f'(x) = 0\) then the slope at that point is 0. This means at that point must be a high or low point. And the second derivative determines which direction the curvature of the function goes.

1.2 What are Taylor Series?

The idea is that every smooth function can be approximated over a speciﬁc range by polynomials that are also smooth.

\[\ \displaystyle \sum _{n \ = \ 0}^{\infty } \ f^{(n)}(x_0) \ \frac {(x \ - \ x_0)^n}{n!},\]

Where \(f^{(n)}\) is the \(n\)-th derivative. With \(x_0\) we can move the approximation along the x coordinate.

You can ﬁnd an more excellent explaination on how to come up with the taylor series here: https://medium.com/@andrew.chamberlain/an-easy-way-to-remember-the-taylor-series-expansion-a7c3f9101063

Just plugging in \(x_0 = 0\) results in a Maclaurin series:

\[\ \displaystyle \sum _{n \ = \ 0}^{\infty } \frac {f^{(n)}(0)}{n!} x^n =f(0)+f'(0)\cdot x+{\frac {f''(0)}{2!}}x^{2}+\dots \]

2 Matrix exponentials

This equation \[\diff {}{t} x(t) = x'(t) = r \cdot x(t)\] can be solved by \[x(t) = e^{rt} \cdot x_0\] where \(x_0\) is some starting position.
This is true because the derivative of \(x(t)\) is always: \[\diff {}{t} x(t) = r \cdot e^{rt} \cdot x_0\]

This pattern can also be applied to higher dimensions:

\begin {equation} \label {eq14} \begin {split} \diff {}{t} \left [ \begin {matrix} x(t) \\y(t) \end {matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin {matrix} a & b \\ c & d \end {matrix}\right ] \cdot \left [ \begin {matrix} x(t) \\ y(t) \end {matrix} \right ] \end {split} \end {equation}

\(\Downarrow \)

\begin {equation} \label {eq15} \begin {split} \left [ \begin {matrix} x(t) \\y(t) \end {matrix} \right ] = e ^{ \left [ \begin {matrix} a & b \\ c & d \end {matrix}\right ] \cdot t} \cdot \left [ \begin {matrix} x_0 \\ y_0 \end {matrix} \right ] \end {split} \end {equation}

Where \(x_0\) and \(y_0\) are some starting position.

Let’s take a look at an example:

Let the derivative of some fuction \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) be:

\begin {equation} \begin {split} x'(t) &= -y(t) \\ y'(t) &= x (t) \end {split} \end {equation} Then we can write the derivatives as a 2d vector:

\[\left [ \begin {matrix} x' \\y' \end {matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin {matrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end {matrix} \right ] \cdot \left [ \begin {matrix} x(t) \\y(t) \end {matrix} \right ]\]

\(\Downarrow \)

\[\left [ \begin {matrix} x(t) \\y(t) \end {matrix} \right ] = e ^{\left [ \begin {matrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end {matrix} \right ] \cdot t} \cdot \left [ \begin {matrix} x_0 \\y_0 \end {matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin {matrix} \cos (t) & -\sin (t)\\\sin (t) & \cos (t) \end {matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin {matrix} x_0 \\y_0 \end {matrix} \right ]\]

Plugging it into the taylor series \(e^X\) will result in \(\cos (t)\) and \(\sin (t)\).

In a vector ﬁeld it looks like this.

Let’s take a look and get a deeper intuition at how this vector ﬁeld is created. For example the vectors around the point \(\left [ \begin {matrix} 5\\5 \end {matrix} \right ]\) points towards the point \(\left [ \begin {matrix} 0\\ 10 \end {matrix} \right ]\). This is because plugging in the point into the derivatives results in: \[\left [ \begin {matrix} x' \\y' \end {matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin {matrix} -y(0) \\x(0) \end {matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin {matrix} -5 \\ 5 \end {matrix} \right ]\] Here \(t = 0\) because it’s our starting point. And \(\left [ \begin {matrix} 5\\5 \end {matrix} \right ]+ \left [ \begin {matrix} -5 \\ 5 \end {matrix} \right ]\) results in \(\left [ \begin {matrix} 0\\10 \end {matrix} \right ]\).
